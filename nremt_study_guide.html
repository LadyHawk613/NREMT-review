Domain 1 — Scene size-up & safety 15–19%

Your first domain on every call. Questions test whether you can identify hazards, determine MOI/NOI, establish scene safety, request resources, and form an initial impression — all before touching the patient.

Core topics

Scene safety

  • BSI/PPE selection based on mechanism and patient presentation
  • Environmental hazards: traffic, violence, unstable structures, utilities
  • Hazmat recognition — ERG basics, placard reading, isolation distances
  • Violence/hostile scene protocols — never enter until LE clears

MOI/NOI

  • Mechanism of injury (MOI): energy transfer, kinematics
  • Nature of illness (NOI): chief complaint, bystander info
  • Significant vs. non-significant MOI (affects spinal precautions)
  • Pediatric vs. adult injury patterns differ — know both

Resource determination

  • Number of patients → MCI activation thresholds
  • ALS vs. BLS need determination from scene info
  • Additional units, specialty teams, helicopter LZ considerations
  • Early activation is never wrong — cancel if not needed

General impression

  • Age, sex, obvious distress, position of patient
  • Life threat identification at a glance
  • Sick vs. not-sick decision: informs pace of the entire call
  • Chief complaint from patient, family, or bystanders
Key mnemonics
6-step scene approach
BSI → Scene safe → MOI/NOI → # patients → Additional resources → C-spine?
Answer every element before patient contact.

High-yield exam tips

Questions in this domain often test whether you protect yourself first. When in doubt, wait for scene control before entering.

On pediatric calls, consider caretaker as source of MOI information — and suspect NAT (non-accidental trauma) when MOI doesn't match injuries.

Spinal precautions are determined by MOI, not by the presence of pain. Significant MOI without complaint still warrants precautions.

Domain 2 — Primary assessment 39–43%

Nearly half the exam. This domain integrates what used to be separate airway, cardiac, and trauma sections. You must demonstrate sequential assessment thinking, immediate life-threat management, and priority patient decisions.

Core sequence: XABCDE

X — Exsanguinating hemorrhage

  • Life-threatening external bleeding: tourniquet first for extremities
  • Wound packing for junctional wounds (axilla, groin, neck)
  • Direct pressure with wound packing for torso
  • Control before airway in true hemorrhagic emergency

A — Airway

  • LOC determines approach: head-tilt/chin-lift vs. jaw thrust
  • OPA sizing: corner of mouth to earlobe
  • NPA sizing: tip of nose to earlobe; contraindicated in suspected skull fx
  • Suction: rigid (Yankauer) for upper airway, flexible for tube/stoma
  • BVM: 1 breath/5–6 sec (adult), 1/3–5 sec (peds)

B — Breathing

  • Rate: normal adult 12–20; assess rate AND quality
  • Tidal volume: look, listen, feel — chest rise must be adequate
  • Adventitious sounds: crackles, wheezes, stridor, gurgling
  • High-flow O2 (15L NRB) for priority patients
  • Tension pneumo: absent breath sounds, tracheal deviation, JVD, hypotension

C — Circulation

  • Pulse: rate, quality, location (radial vs. carotid indicates BP level)
  • Skin: color, temperature, moisture (cool/pale/diaphoretic = shock)
  • Cap refill: <2 sec normal; >2 sec in children = significant
  • Reassess major bleeding after X interventions

D — Disability (neuro)

  • AVPU: Alert / Voice / Pain / Unresponsive
  • GCS rapid estimate: best response to verbal command
  • Pupils: PERRL vs. unequal, dilated, or fixed (herniation sign)
  • Stroke screen: Cincinnati (facial droop, arm drift, speech) or FAST

E — Expose / Environment

  • Expose injuries without compromising dignity or temperature
  • Prevent hypothermia: blankets after assessment
  • Look for medic-alert jewelry, implanted devices, PICC lines
Priority patient criteria

Any of the following = load and go, limit on-scene time to <10 min: poor general impression, unresponsive/no gag, responsive only to pain, difficulty breathing, shock signs, complicated childbirth, chest pain with BP <100, uncontrolled bleeding, altered mental status, severe pain anywhere.

High-yield exam tips

The NREMT expects you to treat life threats as you find them, not after the full assessment. If airway is compromised during A — fix it before moving to B.

Pediatric questions emphasize that respiratory failure leads to cardiac arrest in children — airway and breathing interventions are more urgent than in adults.

Absence of a radial pulse suggests systolic BP below ~80 mmHg — a clinical clue the NREMT uses frequently.

On TEI drag-and-drop questions, you may be asked to sequence an assessment or treatment — practice the order until it's automatic.

Domain 3 — Secondary assessment 5–9%

Smallest domain by weight — but straightforward points. Focus on SAMPLE history, OPQRST, head-to-toe exam technique, and ongoing reassessment.

Core assessment frameworks

SAMPLE history

  • Signs and symptoms (current)
  • Allergies (medications, environmental, food)
  • Medications (Rx, OTC, supplements, illicit)
  • Pertinent past history
  • Last oral intake (time of last meal/drink)
  • Events leading to call

OPQRST (pain assessment)

  • Onset — when and how did it start?
  • Provocation/palliation — better or worse with?
  • Quality — sharp, pressure, burning, tearing
  • Radiation — does it move anywhere?
  • Severity — 1–10 scale; compare to baseline
  • Time — duration, intermittent vs. constant

Rapid head-to-toe exam

  • DCAP-BTLS at each region: deformities, contusions, abrasions, punctures, burns, tenderness, lacerations, swelling
  • Head: PERRL, facial symmetry, Battle's sign, raccoon eyes
  • Neck: JVD, tracheal position, subcutaneous emphysema
  • Chest: equal rise, paradoxical motion, bilateral breath sounds
  • Abdomen: rigidity, distension, guarding, evisceration
  • Pelvis: instability (assess once only — do not rock repeatedly)
  • Extremities: PMS — pulse, motor, sensation distal to injury
  • Posterior: log-roll and assess if spinal injury possible

Baseline vital signs

  • Respirations: rate and quality
  • Pulse: rate and quality
  • Skin: color, temperature, moisture
  • BP: systolic and diastolic; compare to patient's baseline
  • SpO2: normal 95–100%; below 94% requires intervention
  • Pupils: PERRL, size in mm, response speed
  • Blood glucose: for AMS patients per protocol
Ongoing reassessment frequency
Stable patient
Reassess every 15 minutes. Repeat primary assessment, vitals, interventions check, and mental status trends.
Unstable / priority patient
Reassess every 5 minutes. Any deterioration triggers reassessment and possible intervention escalation.
High-yield exam tips

Questions here often ask about the sequence of assessment vs. treatment. The NREMT expects you to identify the problem before treating — "assess first" is almost always the right answer.

Battle's sign and raccoon eyes are late signs — they indicate prior head injury and won't appear immediately at the scene.

Pelvic instability: assess once with gentle lateral compression. Repeated assessment risks worsening internal hemorrhage.

Domain 4 — Patient treatment & transport 20–24%

Second largest domain. Covers clinical interventions, condition-specific care, and transport decisions. Content from former Airway, Cardiac, Trauma, and Medical/OB domains lives here now.

Respiratory & airway interventions

Airway adjuncts

  • OPA: unconscious, no gag reflex; insert inverted in adults, direct in peds
  • NPA: conscious or semi-conscious; can use with gag reflex
  • Suction: max 15 sec adults, 10 sec peds per attempt
  • Supraglottic airways: know your local protocol scope

Oxygen delivery

  • Nasal cannula: 1–6 LPM, 24–44% FiO2
  • Simple face mask: 6–10 LPM, 35–60% FiO2
  • NRB: 10–15 LPM, 60–90% FiO2 — preferred for respiratory distress
  • BVM with O2: near 100% FiO2 with proper seal
  • CPAP: where authorized — cardiogenic pulmonary edema, severe asthma
Cardiac emergencies

Cardiac arrest / CPR

  • Adult: 30:2, 100–120/min, 2–2.4 in depth, full recoil
  • 2-rescuer peds: 15:2; infant: 2-finger or 2-thumb encircling
  • AED: attach as soon as available — minimize CPR interruption
  • Analyze → shock if advised → 2 min CPR → re-analyze
  • Do not stop CPR to check pulse unless AED advises "no shock"

ACS / chest pain

  • Aspirin 324 mg (if not allergic, no contraindications, per protocol)
  • Nitroglycerin: BP >100 systolic, no PDE-5 inhibitor use in 24–48 hr
  • Position of comfort; oxygen if SpO2 <94%
  • 12-lead acquisition if in scope — transmit early alert if available
  • Rapid transport — time-sensitive intervention at hospital
Trauma care

Hemorrhage control

  • Direct pressure: first line for most wounds
  • Tourniquet: extremity bleeding uncontrolled; document time of application
  • Wound packing + direct pressure: junctional wounds (groin, axilla, neck)
  • Hemostatic gauze: QuikClot, Combat Gauze — protocol-dependent

Shock management

  • Position: supine; keep warm with blankets
  • High-flow O2
  • Rapid transport: definitive treatment is surgical
  • IV fluids: ALS or protocol-dependent at EMT level

Musculoskeletal injuries

  • Splint in position found (unless neurovascular compromise)
  • Assess PMS before and after splinting
  • Traction splint: mid-shaft femur fx only; contraindicated in hip, knee, ankle, open fx, pelvis
  • Long backboard vs. scoop stretcher: know indications

Burns

  • Stop burning: remove smoldering clothing, cool with room-temp water ≤10 min
  • Rule of Nines adults: head 9%, each arm 9%, each leg 18%, ant. trunk 18%, post. trunk 18%, perineum 1%
  • Peds: head = 18%, each leg = 14%
  • Burn center criteria: face/hands/feet/genitalia, circumferential, electrical/chemical, >10% BSA partial-thickness
  • Cover with dry sterile dressings; no ice or creams
Medical emergencies

Diabetic emergencies

  • Hypoglycemia: AMS + low glucose → oral glucose if conscious/able to swallow
  • Hyperglycemia: gradual onset, Kussmaul respirations, fruity breath — transport; no field correction
  • When in doubt: treat for hypoglycemia

Seizures

  • Protect from injury — do not restrain, nothing in mouth
  • Lateral position after seizure (recovery position)
  • Postictal: decreased responsiveness expected — monitor airway
  • Status epilepticus: ≥5 min or no recovery between seizures — urgent transport
  • Febrile seizures in children: usually self-limited; still transport

Anaphylaxis

  • Epinephrine auto-injector: 0.3 mg adult, 0.15 mg peds (<30 kg)
  • Injection site: outer mid-thigh, through clothing if necessary
  • Second dose at 5–15 min if no improvement and transport time allows
  • Diphenhydramine: adjunct only — never replaces epi
  • Supine + legs elevated if hypotension; sitting if respiratory distress

Stroke

  • Cincinnati/FAST — positive screen = stroke until proven otherwise
  • Last known well = time zero for thrombolytics
  • Do not give glucose unless confirmed hypoglycemia
  • Maintain airway; O2 if SpO2 <94%; rapid transport to stroke center
  • Pre-notify hospital with stroke alert

OB/GYN emergencies

  • Normal delivery: support head, guide body, do not pull
  • Shoulder dystocia: McRoberts maneuver + suprapubic pressure
  • Cord prolapse: elevate presenting part with gloved hand; transport
  • Breech: support but do not pull; create airway space if head trapped
  • Eclampsia: seizure in 3rd trimester — protect, transport, avoid stimulation

Behavioral emergencies

  • Safety first: de-escalate, never turn your back, maintain exit access
  • Restraint: minimum necessary force; protocol-based; document thoroughly
  • Excited delirium: hyperthermia, cardiac arrest risk — urgent ALS/hospital
  • Rule out organic causes: hypoglycemia, hypoxia, head injury
Environmental emergencies

Heat emergencies

  • Heat cramps: muscle cramps, normal AMS → rest, oral fluids, cool environment
  • Heat exhaustion: heavy sweating, weakness, cool/moist skin → cool, supine, fluids
  • Heat stroke: hot skin, AMS, high core temp → rapid cooling, immediate transport

Cold emergencies

  • Hypothermia: mild (32–35°C) = shivering, alert; severe (<32°C) = no shivering, AMS, arrhythmias
  • Handle gently — cold heart is irritable; rough movement causes V-fib
  • Remove wet clothing; passive rewarming; do not rub extremities
  • Frostbite: do not rewarm if refreezing possible; protect, pad, transport
High-yield exam tips

For ACS: nitroglycerin contraindications — systolic <100, PDE-5 inhibitor use within 24–48 hr, inferior MI with RV involvement.

Traction splint questions: if the answer includes a knee injury or open fracture with bone protrusion — traction splint is contraindicated.

On OB questions, the NREMT almost always expects you to transport rather than deliver on scene if there is time.

Domain 5 — Operations 9–13%

Covers EMS system functions: ICS, MCI, documentation, lifting/moving, vehicle ops, and EMT wellness. Reliable points — memorize the structures and criteria.

Core topics

Incident command system (ICS)

  • Incident Commander: overall scene authority
  • Unified Command: multi-agency events
  • NIMS: standardized ICS — National Incident Management System
  • Span of control: 3–7 subordinates per supervisor (optimal 5)
  • Sections: Operations, Logistics, Finance/Admin, Planning

MCI triage — START

  • Assess: respirations → perfusion → mental status
  • Black (expectant): no breathing after repositioning, or clearly dead
  • Red (immediate): RR >30 or <10, radial pulse absent, fails commands
  • Yellow (delayed): follows commands, perfusing, RR 10–29
  • Green (minor): ambulatory — "the walking wounded"
  • JumpSTART for peds: 15 rescue breaths before black tag

Patient movement

  • Emergency moves: immediate life threat to patient or EMT
  • Non-urgent moves: proper technique, spine protection, equipment
  • Body mechanics: legs not back, keep load close, do not twist
  • Power grip: palms up, all fingers engaged, thumbs apart
  • Power lift: feet shoulder-width, back straight, lean into carry
  • Stair chair: preferred for ambulatory patients on stairs

Documentation

  • PCR: legal document — complete, accurate, timely
  • Errors: single line through, initial, date — never white-out
  • Pertinent negatives: document what is absent and relevant
  • Refusal of care: competent adult, fully informed, signed

Air medical considerations

  • LZ size: 100×100 ft minimum, clear of obstacles and wires
  • Land/take off into the wind when possible
  • Never approach from the rear (tail rotor)
  • Approach only from front 180° when crew signals
  • Altitude: decreased PaO2, gas expansion (pneumothorax risk)

EMT wellness & safety

  • Standard precautions on every patient, every time
  • N95 for suspected airborne (TB); surgical mask for droplet
  • Decontamination: patient and equipment after hazmat exposure
  • CISM/CISD: critical incident stress management after traumatic events
High-yield exam tips

START triage questions will give you a scenario — practice applying respiratory → perfusion → mental status until automatic.

Documentation error correction: single line through, initial, and date is the only legally correct method.

For helicopter LZ questions: never approach from the rear regardless of rotor status — this is an absolute.

Technology enhanced items (TEIs) Scored since April 7, 2025

TEIs replace some traditional multiple-choice questions. BLS candidates do NOT get clinical judgment scenarios — those are AEMT/Paramedic only.

TEI formats you will encounter

Drag and drop

Place items in the correct sequence or category. Common for assessment order, treatment priority, or triage classification. Think skill-sheet sequence.

Build a list

Select and order correct items from a longer list. Often used for prioritizing interventions or listing assessment steps. Order matters.

Option box / multi-select

Select all correct answers. Read all options before clicking. Common for contraindications, signs of a condition, or correct interventions.

Multiple response

Presented as checkboxes. Do not assume only one answer is correct — read the stem for "select all that apply" language.

TEI strategy

Drag-and-drop: default to your skill-sheet sequence. The NREMT sequences mirror the psychomotor skill sheets.

Build-a-list: think "what would I do first, second, third on scene?" — the priority order that governs your scene approach governs the list.

Multi-select/option box: eliminate clearly wrong answers first, then select all remaining that are correct.

Practice TEIs before test day — the format itself can cost time if it feels unfamiliar. Limmer EMTReview and Pocket Prep both now include TEI practice.

About CAT scoring

The exam stops when the computer reaches 95% statistical confidence that you are above or below the passing standard. Harder questions mean you are performing well — the algorithm gives harder items to candidates above passing level. Most candidates see 70–100 questions. Do not interpret question difficulty or quantity as a sign of failure.
